# 1. Originality Statement & Concept Positioning

## 1.1 Conceptual Inspiration

The genesis of this course draws upon a single, compelling premise encountered in Krish Ashok's *Masala Lab* — that everyday cooking is not merely a procedural task but a space where scientific reasoning, sensory judgment, and adaptive decision-making converge. The book illustrates that home cooks routinely engage with principles of chemistry, physics, and biology without recognising them as such.

From this starting point, five high-level inspiration themes were extracted — none of which are unique to any single text, but which together form the conceptual backbone of this course:

1. **Scientific reasoning in everyday practice** — the idea that ordinary domestic actions embody testable principles.
2. **Experimentation as a learning stance** — that cooking outcomes improve not through memorising recipes but through deliberate variation and observation.
3. **Variability and uncertainty** — that identical procedures can yield different outcomes depending on context, materials, and environment, and that managing this variability is a core competency.
4. **Sensory awareness as an analytical tool** — that trained observation (colour, aroma, texture, sound) constitutes a form of data collection and interpretation.
5. **Principle-based understanding over procedural replication** — that understanding *why* something works enables transfer to novel situations.

## 1.2 How Originality Was Ensured

This course does **not** replicate the source material in any of the following ways:

| Dimension | Source Material Approach | This Course's Approach |
|-----------|------------------------|----------------------|
| **Structure** | Organised by culinary-science topics (heat, spice, browning, acids) | Organised by cognitive development stages (observation → hypothesis → experimentation → reflection → adaptation) |
| **Sequencing** | Disciplinary chapters following food science categories | Learning progression modelled on Bloom's revised taxonomy, moving from lower-order to higher-order thinking |
| **Examples** | Specific Indian recipes and cooking scenarios from the author's experience | Learner-generated examples from their own kitchens, cultures, and dietary contexts |
| **Frameworks** | Informal science communication narrative | Formal instructional design grounded in constructivist theory, experiential learning, and problem-centred instruction |
| **Assessment** | Quiz-based factual recall (as seen in the Bookey summary) | Performance-based authentic assessments requiring reasoning, interpretation, and experimental design |
| **Voice** | Popular science writing aimed at entertainment and information | Facilitated learning experience designed for cognitive transformation |

The course uses cooking as a **pedagogical context**, not as content to be transmitted. The distinction is critical: learners are not studying food science — they are developing transferable reasoning skills *through* the medium of cooking.

## 1.3 Unique Instructional Positioning

**Course Title:** *The Thinking Kitchen: Reasoning, Experimentation, and Adaptive Decision-Making Through Everyday Cooking*

**Positioning Statement:** This course occupies a space between informal adult learning and applied critical thinking education. It is not a culinary skills course, nor a food science programme. It is an interdisciplinary learning experience that uses the universally accessible context of home cooking to develop three core competencies:

1. **Analytical reasoning** — the ability to observe a process, identify variables, and explain outcomes using evidence.
2. **Experimental thinking** — the capacity to design simple tests, control variables, and draw conclusions from results.
3. **Adaptive decision-making** — the skill to modify approaches in real-time based on sensory feedback, contextual constraints, and prior experience.

The cognitive transformation the course targets is:

> **Procedural imitation → Analytical reasoning → Adaptive experimentation**

This positions the course as a *thinking skills programme delivered through a cooking context*, rather than a cooking course with scientific content added.

# 2. Course Concept & Rationale

## 2.1 The Problem This Course Addresses

Most people learn to cook by following instructions — recipes, family demonstrations, video tutorials. This procedural approach works until something goes wrong: the dough doesn't rise, the sauce splits, the spices taste flat. At that point, the procedural cook is stranded, because they were never taught to reason about *why* processes behave as they do.

This gap — between procedural competence and principled understanding — mirrors a broader educational challenge. In many domains, learners can execute routines without understanding underlying principles, leaving them unable to adapt, troubleshoot, or innovate.

## 2.2 Why Cooking as a Learning Context

Cooking is uniquely suited as a vehicle for developing reasoning and experimentation skills because:

- **Universal accessibility** — every learner has access to a kitchen, however modest.
- **Immediate feedback loops** — outcomes are visible, tangible, and sensory within minutes.
- **Low stakes** — failed experiments are edible (or at least harmless), reducing anxiety.
- **High variability** — ingredients, equipment, altitude, humidity, and personal taste create natural variation that demands adaptive thinking.
- **Cultural resonance** — food connects to identity, memory, and community, providing intrinsic motivation.

## 2.3 Design Philosophy

The course is grounded in four complementary pedagogical commitments:

- **Constructivism** — Learners build knowledge through personal experimentation and reflection, not through information transmission. The instructor's role is to scaffold inquiry, not deliver answers.
- **Experiential Learning (Kolb)** — Each module cycles through concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualisation, and active experimentation.
- **Problem-Centred Instruction (Merrill)** — Learning is organised around authentic problems (e.g., "Why did this fail?", "How would you adjust for a different outcome?") rather than topics.
- **Reflective Practice (Schön)** — Learners are expected to articulate their reasoning, examine their assumptions, and document their evolving understanding through structured reflection.

# 3. Learning Objectives

## 3.1 Terminal Objective

> By the end of this seven-week course, learners will be able to **analyse everyday cooking processes by identifying underlying variables, designing simple controlled experiments, and making reasoned adaptive decisions** when faced with unfamiliar or variable cooking situations — demonstrating their reasoning through documented experimentation, reflective writing, and peer discussion in an asynchronous online environment.

**Bloom's Level:** Analyse / Evaluate / Create (higher-order thinking)
**SMART Alignment:** Specific (cooking processes, variables, experiments); Measurable (documented outputs); Achievable (home kitchen, no special equipment); Relevant (everyday cooking situations); Time-bound (seven weeks).

## 3.2 Enabling Objectives

| # | Enabling Objective | Bloom's Level | Taxonomy Rationale |
|---|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| EO1 | Identify and describe at least three observable variables (e.g., temperature, time, proportion) that influence the outcome of a common cooking process, using structured observation. | **Understand** | Learners must first comprehend that cooking outcomes are governed by identifiable variables before they can manipulate them. This establishes foundational analytical vocabulary. |
| EO2 | Explain how changes in a single variable produce different cooking outcomes, drawing on evidence from personal kitchen experiments. | **Analyse** | Moves beyond description to causal reasoning — learners must isolate a variable and articulate its effect, demonstrating analytical thinking. |
| EO3 | Design a simple controlled experiment to test a hypothesis about a cooking process, specifying the variable being tested, the control condition, and the method of observation. | **Create** | Requires learners to synthesise their understanding into an original experimental plan, demonstrating the capacity to generate knowledge rather than consume it. |
| EO4 | Evaluate competing explanations for why a cooking process succeeded or failed, using sensory evidence and reasoning rather than recipe authority. | **Evaluate** | Develops critical judgment — learners must weigh evidence, consider alternatives, and justify their interpretation rather than defaulting to received wisdom. |
| EO5 | Adapt a familiar cooking process to an unfamiliar constraint (different ingredient, equipment, or dietary requirement) and justify the adaptations made using principled reasoning. | **Create / Evaluate** | The culmination of adaptive decision-making — learners must transfer principles to novel contexts and defend their choices. |
| EO6 | Communicate experimental findings and reflective insights clearly to peers through written posts and structured feedback in an asynchronous online forum. | **Apply** | Ensures learners can articulate reasoning to others, reinforcing their own understanding and contributing to a community of inquiry. |

# 4. Module Blueprint

The course is structured across **seven modules** that reflect a cognitive development arc rather than disciplinary categories. Each module builds on the previous one, moving learners progressively from structured observation to independent adaptive experimentation.

## Module 1: Seeing What You Already Know
**Duration:** Week 1

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To surface learners' existing cooking knowledge and shift their perspective from "following instructions" to "observing processes." |
| **Key Learning Focus** | Structured observation; identifying what happens during cooking and why it might matter. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From unconscious routine → conscious observation. |
| **Enabling Objectives Addressed** | EO1 |
| **Online Feasibility** | Learners photograph/video their own cooking at home; text-based reflections submitted asynchronously; discussion forums for sharing observations. No special equipment needed. |

## Module 2: The Language of Variables
**Duration:** Week 2

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To introduce the concept of variables in cooking — what changes, what stays the same, and what you can control. |
| **Key Learning Focus** | Identifying independent, dependent, and controlled variables in everyday cooking contexts. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From "things just happen" → "outcomes depend on identifiable factors." |
| **Enabling Objectives Addressed** | EO1, EO2 |
| **Online Feasibility** | Simple at-home comparisons (e.g., same dish with two different timing durations); photo documentation; asynchronous peer sharing of variable lists. Requires only everyday kitchen items. |

## Module 3: Why Did That Happen? Building Explanations
**Duration:** Week 3

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To develop learners' capacity to move from observation to explanation — constructing causal accounts of cooking outcomes. |
| **Key Learning Focus** | Causal reasoning; distinguishing correlation from causation in cooking; forming hypotheses. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From describing *what* happened → explaining *why* it happened. |
| **Enabling Objectives Addressed** | EO2, EO4 |
| **Online Feasibility** | Scenario-based analysis tasks (provided via LMS); learners write explanatory posts about their own cooking experiences; peer feedback on reasoning quality. No equipment beyond a standard kitchen. |

## Module 4: Designing Your Own Experiment
**Duration:** Week 4

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To scaffold learners through the process of designing and conducting a simple, controlled cooking experiment. |
| **Key Learning Focus** | Experimental design fundamentals — hypothesis, variable isolation, control conditions, observation methods. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From passively experiencing outcomes → actively testing ideas. |
| **Enabling Objectives Addressed** | EO3 |
| **Online Feasibility** | Learners design and execute an experiment in their own kitchen using ingredients they already have. Experiment plans submitted for peer and facilitator feedback before execution. Documentation through photos, notes, or short video. |

## Module 5: Reading the Signals — Sensory Evidence as Data
**Duration:** Week 5

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To train learners to use their senses — sight, smell, touch, sound, taste — as systematic tools for gathering evidence about cooking processes. |
| **Key Learning Focus** | Sensory observation protocols; articulating qualitative data; linking sensory cues to process states. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From casual tasting → disciplined sensory analysis. |
| **Enabling Objectives Addressed** | EO1, EO4 |
| **Online Feasibility** | At-home sensory exercises with common ingredients (e.g., comparing aroma before and after heating a spice, describing texture changes during cooking). Structured observation worksheets provided digitally. Peer discussion of sensory vocabulary. |

**Duration:** Week 6

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To develop learners' capacity to diagnose problems, evaluate competing explanations, and adapt their approach under constraints. |
| **Key Learning Focus** | Troubleshooting reasoning; adaptive decision-making; working within constraints (substitutions, equipment limitations, dietary needs). |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From "it failed, throw it away" → "it failed, here's why, and here's what I'd change." |
| **Enabling Objectives Addressed** | EO4, EO5 |
| **Online Feasibility** | Learners receive (or generate) "failure scenarios" and propose diagnostic reasoning and adaptations. One at-home challenge: deliberately constrain a familiar process (e.g., missing a key ingredient) and document the adaptation. Asynchronous peer troubleshooting discussions. |

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To bring together all prior learning into an integrated capstone experience where learners demonstrate adaptive, principled cooking reasoning. |
| **Key Learning Focus** | Integration of observation, explanation, experimentation, sensory analysis, and adaptation into a coherent personal practice. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From guided reasoning → independent adaptive experimentation. |
| **Enabling Objectives Addressed** | EO3, EO4, EO5, EO6 |
| **Online Feasibility** | Capstone project: learners select a cooking challenge, design and execute an experiment, document the process, and present findings in a reflective portfolio submitted via LMS. Peer review of portfolios. All work conducted in learners' own kitchens with available materials. |

# 5. Learning Activities (Online Adapted)

## Module 1: Seeing What You Already Know

### Asynchronous Learning Activities
- **Kitchen Audit Activity:** Learners record a 2–3 minute video or take a series of photographs of themselves preparing a routine dish, narrating what they are doing and why. They then review their own footage and write a 300-word reflection identifying three moments where they made a decision without thinking about it.
- **"What I Never Noticed" Journal Entry:** Learners cook the same dish they've made many times but commit to observing one element they usually ignore (e.g., how the colour of onions changes over time, what the oil does when heated). They record observations in a digital learning journal hosted on the LMS.

### Interactive Tools / Simulations
- **Observation Checklist (Interactive PDF/Form):** A structured checklist provided via the LMS prompting learners to observe and note specific sensory changes (visual, auditory, olfactory, tactile) during cooking. Embedded in Nearpod or PearDeck for interactivity.

### Reflection Tasks
- **Guided Reflection Prompt:** "Before this module, I cooked by ___. After completing these activities, I now notice ___. The biggest surprise was ___." Submitted as a discussion forum post.

### Peer Interaction
- **Forum Discussion — "Things We Never Noticed":** Learners share their most surprising observation and comment on at least two peers' posts, identifying similarities or differences in what others noticed.

---

## Module 2: The Language of Variables

### Asynchronous Learning Activities
- **Variable Hunt:** Learners select a simple cooking process (e.g., boiling an egg, toasting bread, brewing tea) and list every factor that could affect the outcome. They categorise these as variables they can control, variables they cannot control, and variables they didn't think of until they looked closely.
- **Side-by-Side Comparison:** Learners cook the same item twice, changing only one variable (e.g., heat level, timing, amount of water), photograph both outcomes, and write a 200-word comparison noting observable differences.

### Interactive Tools / Simulations
- **Variable Sorting Activity (Drag-and-Drop):** An interactive sorting exercise built in Genially or Articulate Rise where learners drag cooking factors into categories: independent variable, dependent variable, controlled variable.

### Reflection Tasks
- **Reflection Prompt:** "Which variable had the biggest impact on my result? Why do I think so? What would I change if I did this again?"

### Peer Interaction
- **Peer Variable Review:** Learners post their variable lists and a peer partner reviews them, suggesting any variables the original author may have missed. This is conducted asynchronously via forum threads.

---

## Module 3: Why Did That Happen? Building Explanations

### Asynchronous Learning Activities
- **Scenario Analysis Task:** Three short written scenarios are provided (e.g., "A learner's bread dough didn't rise. They used warm water, yeast, flour, and salt. What might explain this?"). Learners write explanatory responses for each, identifying possible causes and justifying their reasoning.
- **Personal "Why" Investigation:** Learners recall a time something went unexpectedly wrong (or right) in their kitchen. They write a 400-word investigative post: What happened? What variables were at play? What is their best explanation?

### Interactive Tools / Simulations
- **Cause-and-Effect Concept Map:** Using a digital concept mapping tool (Mindmap or LMS-embedded), learners create a visual map connecting a cooking outcome to its possible causes.

### Reflection Tasks
- **Reflection Prompt:** "How confident am I in my explanation? What evidence supports it? What alternative explanations did I consider and reject — and why?"

### Peer Interaction
- **Peer Hypothesis Challenge:** Learners read a peer's "Why" investigation and post a constructive alternative explanation. The original author then responds, either integrating the alternative or defending their original reasoning with evidence.

---

## Module 4: Designing Your Own Experiment

### Asynchronous Learning Activities
- **Experiment Design Worksheet:** Learners complete a structured template: research question, hypothesis, independent variable, dependent variable, controlled variables, method, observation plan. Submitted to the LMS for feedback before execution.
- **Kitchen Experiment Execution:** After receiving feedback, learners conduct their experiment at home, documenting the process with photos, notes, and/or short video clips. They then write up findings in a structured format: What I did → What I observed → What I conclude → What I would do differently.

### Interactive Tools / Simulations
- **Experiment Design Builder (Scaffolded Form):** An interactive, step-by-step form built in Articulate Rise or PearDeck that guides learners through the experiment design process with embedded tips and examples from non-cooking domains to avoid leading their thinking.

### Reflection Tasks
- **Post-Experiment Reflection:** "Did my results match my hypothesis? If not, what surprised me? What did I learn about the process of experimenting itself?"

### Peer Interaction
- **Peer Experiment Review:** Learners exchange experiment plans before execution and provide feedback on clarity, feasibility, and variable control. After execution, they share results and peers comment on whether the conclusions seem well-supported.

---

## Module 5: Reading the Signals — Sensory Evidence as Data

### Asynchronous Learning Activities
- **Sensory Observation Protocol Exercise:** Learners select a cooking process that involves a visible transformation (e.g., caramelising sugar, wilting greens, reducing a sauce). Using a provided sensory observation sheet, they systematically record what they see, hear, smell, and feel at three points: beginning, midpoint, and end.
- **Sensory Vocabulary Builder:** Learners read a short curated resource on descriptive sensory language (not from the source text) and then write a "sensory profile" of a dish they know well, using at least ten specific descriptive terms.

### Interactive Tools / Simulations
- **Sensory Matching Quiz:** An interactive quiz (via Kahoot or Wordwall) where learners match sensory descriptions (e.g., "nutty aroma," "glossy surface," "sizzling sound") to cooking process stages.

### Reflection Tasks
- **Reflection Prompt:** "How has paying deliberate attention to sensory cues changed the way I cook? Can I identify a moment where a sensory signal told me something important about my food that I would have missed before?"

### Peer Interaction
- **Sensory Description Swap:** Learners write a purely sensory description of a cooking moment (no process names, no ingredient labels — only what they perceived) and post it. Peers try to guess the process and stage, then discuss how accurately sensory language communicated the experience.

---

## Module 6: When Things Go Wrong — Troubleshooting and Adaptation

### Asynchronous Learning Activities
- **Failure Case Study Analysis:** Three detailed failure scenarios are provided (with photos). Learners diagnose the probable cause, propose at least two possible explanations, evaluate which is most likely, and suggest corrective adaptations.
- **Constraint Challenge:** Learners select a dish they know how to make and remove one key element (a specific ingredient, a piece of equipment, or a step). They then plan and execute an adapted version, documenting their reasoning for each substitution or modification.

### Interactive Tools / Simulations
- **Troubleshooting Decision Tree (Interactive):** An interactive branching scenario built in Articulate Storyline or Genially where learners navigate a cooking failure by making diagnostic choices at each branch point, receiving feedback on their reasoning.

### Reflection Tasks
- **Reflection Prompt:** "What did the constraint force me to think about that I normally take for granted? How did I decide what to substitute and what to keep?"

### Peer Interaction
- **Peer Troubleshooting Forum:** Learners post their "Constraint Challenge" results and peers evaluate whether the adaptations were well-reasoned. Discussion focuses on *reasoning quality*, not whether the dish tasted good.

---

## Module 7: The Adaptive Cook — Integration and Transfer

### Asynchronous Learning Activities
- **Capstone Project — The Thinking Kitchen Portfolio:** Learners select a cooking challenge that is personally meaningful and unfamiliar (a dish they've never made, a technique they've never tried, or a dietary constraint they've never navigated). They design an experiment, execute it, document the process, analyse the results, and reflect on what they learned — compiling everything into a digital portfolio.
- **Portfolio Components:** (1) A brief context statement, (2) An experiment design, (3) Process documentation with photos/video, (4) A results analysis, (5) A reflective essay (500–700 words) on their cognitive journey through the course.

### Interactive Tools / Simulations
- **Portfolio Template (LMS-hosted):** A structured submission template within the LMS (Google Classroom or Canvas) with clearly labelled sections, embedded rubric, and exemplar annotations showing quality indicators.

### Reflection Tasks
- **Course-Level Reflection:** "How has my relationship with cooking changed? What reasoning skills have I developed that I could apply outside the kitchen?"

### Peer Interaction
- **Peer Portfolio Review:** Each learner reviews one peer's portfolio using a provided rubric focused on reasoning quality, evidence use, and reflective depth. Reviews are submitted as structured written feedback.

# 6. Assessment Strategy & Mapping

## 6.1 Assessment Philosophy

Assessment in this course is designed around **constructive alignment** (Biggs, 1996): every assessment directly measures the learning objectives it targets, using the same type of cognitive activity that the learning experiences develop. Learners are never assessed on factual recall they were not asked to develop, nor on skills they were not given opportunity to practise.

All assessments are **authentic** — they require learners to perform the actual reasoning, experimentation, and reflection that the course objectives describe, using evidence from their own kitchens.

## 6.2 Formative Assessment Map

| Module | Formative Assessment | What It Measures | Aligned Objective |
|--------|---------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| M1 | Kitchen Audit Reflection + Forum Post | Quality of observation; ability to notice previously unconscious decisions | EO1 |
| M2 | Side-by-Side Comparison Write-Up | Ability to identify and describe variables; quality of comparative observation | EO1, EO2 |
| M3 | Scenario Analysis + Peer Hypothesis Challenge | Causal reasoning quality; ability to generate and evaluate explanations | EO2, EO4 |
| M4 | Experiment Design Worksheet (pre-execution feedback) | Quality of experimental design; clarity of hypothesis and variable control | EO3 |
| M4 | Experiment Write-Up | Quality of evidence-based conclusions; honesty about unexpected results | EO3, EO4 |
| M5 | Sensory Observation Protocol + Sensory Description Swap | Precision of sensory observation; ability to communicate qualitative data | EO1, EO4 |
| M6 | Failure Case Study Analysis + Constraint Challenge | Diagnostic reasoning; quality of adaptive decision-making under constraints | EO4, EO5 |
| M6 | Peer Troubleshooting Forum Contributions | Quality of feedback; ability to evaluate others' reasoning | EO4, EO6 |

**Feedback Mechanism:** Formative assessments receive facilitator feedback within 48 hours and structured peer feedback within each module's discussion cycle. Rubric criteria are shared in advance so learners understand quality expectations.

## 6.3 Summative Assessment

### Capstone: The Thinking Kitchen Portfolio (Module 7)

| Component | Weight | What It Measures | Aligned Objectives |
|-----------|--------|------------------|-------------------|
| Experiment Design | 20% | Rigour of hypothesis, variable identification, and method planning | EO3 |
| Process Documentation | 15% | Quality and thoroughness of observational evidence | EO1, EO4 |
| Results Analysis | 25% | Depth of evidence-based reasoning; honesty about unexpected outcomes | EO2, EO4 |
| Reflective Essay | 25% | Metacognitive awareness; articulation of cognitive growth; quality of self-assessment | EO4, EO5 |
| Peer Review Contribution | 15% | Quality of feedback provided to a peer's portfolio; constructiveness and specificity | EO6 |

### Summative Assessment Rubric Criteria (abbreviated)

| Criterion | Excellent | Competent | Developing |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Reasoning Quality** | Explanations are evidence-based, consider alternatives, and demonstrate nuanced understanding of variables | Explanations reference evidence but may miss alternative explanations | Explanations are superficial or rely on recipe authority rather than evidence |
| **Experimental Rigour** | Clear hypothesis, well-controlled variables, systematic observation, honest reporting of unexpected results | Experiment is structured but variable control or observation may have gaps | Experiment lacks clear hypothesis or variable control |
| **Adaptive Thinking** | Demonstrates principled adaptation with clear justification; transfers reasoning to novel contexts | Adaptations are reasonable but justification may be incomplete | Adaptations are arbitrary or poorly justified |
| **Reflective Depth** | Reflection demonstrates genuine metacognitive awareness and articulates specific cognitive shifts | Reflection describes experiences but may not fully articulate *what changed* in thinking | Reflection is descriptive rather than analytical |
| **Peer Engagement** | Feedback to peers is specific, constructive, and demonstrates the ability to evaluate others' reasoning | Feedback is relevant but may lack specificity | Feedback is generic or superficial |

## Differentiation Strategy

Learning has been differentiated across the course through the following approaches:

- **Choice and autonomy:** Learners select their own dishes, experiments, and challenges throughout the course, allowing them to work within their cultural context, dietary practices, skill level, and available resources.

## 9.1 Analytical Framework

Tool selection for this course is grounded in two complementary frameworks:

**TPACK (Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge):** Tools are selected not merely for their features but for their capacity to support the intersection of technology, pedagogy, and content. Each tool must enhance the specific pedagogical approach (constructivist, experiential, problem-centred) within the specific content domain (reasoning through cooking), not merely digitise a traditional activity.

**SAMR Model (Substitution, Augmentation, Modification, Redefinition):** Tools are evaluated based on whether they merely substitute analogue activities (S), augment them with functional improvement (A), significantly modify the learning task (M), or redefine what is possible in ways that would be inconceivable without technology (R). This course prioritises tools operating at the Modification and Redefinition levels wherever possible.

# 10. Instructional Design Theory Application

## 10.1 Instructional Design Model: Merrill's First Principles of Instruction

While several ID models were considered (ADDIE as process framework, Gagné's Nine Events for session-level design, UDL for accessibility), **Merrill's First Principles of Instruction** was selected as the primary design model because it aligns most naturally with the course's commitment to problem-centred, experiential learning. Merrill's framework is not a process model — it is a set of design principles that can be applied regardless of development methodology. This makes it particularly well-suited for a course that values authentic reasoning over information delivery.

### How Merrill's First Principles Informed This Course

**Principle 1 — Problem-Centred: Learning is promoted when learners are engaged in solving real-world problems.**

This principle is the foundation of the entire course architecture. Rather than organising content by topic (which would reproduce the disciplinary chapter structure of food science texts), the course is organised around a series of increasingly complex *problems* that learners encounter in their own kitchens.

- Module 1 poses the implicit problem: "Why do I cook the way I do, and what am I not noticing?"
- Module 3 poses: "Something happened — why?"
- Module 4 poses: "I have a question — how do I find out?"
- Module 6 poses: "Something went wrong — how do I diagnose and fix it?"
- Module 7 poses: "I face an unfamiliar challenge — how do I adapt?"

Each module is structured around a problem that requires the cognitive skills being developed, not around content to be memorised.

**Principle 2 — Activation: Learning is promoted when existing knowledge is activated as a foundation for new knowledge.**

Module 1 is entirely dedicated to activation. The Kitchen Audit activity and "What I Never Noticed" journal are designed to surface learners' existing (often tacit) cooking knowledge before any new concepts are introduced. This ensures that subsequent modules build on what learners already know and do, rather than starting from an assumed blank slate.

Throughout the course, each module begins with a connection prompt or a brief recall activity that links new learning to what was developed in previous modules.

**Principle 3 — Demonstration: Learning is promoted when new knowledge is demonstrated to the learner.**

Demonstration in this course takes three forms:

1. **Facilitator modelling:** The Module 4 orientation video demonstrates the process of turning a question into an experiment — but critically, the facilitator models *thinking*, not expertise.
2. **Scaffolded walkthroughs:** The interactive lessons in Articulate Rise and PearDeck demonstrate the process of variable identification, hypothesis formation, and experimental design using non-cooking examples, so learners see the reasoning process without having their cooking experiments predetermined.
3. **Peer demonstration:** Forum discussions throughout the course allow learners to see how peers approach the same tasks, providing multiple demonstrations of reasoning approaches.

**Principle 4 — Application: Learning is promoted when learners apply new knowledge.**

Every module requires application — not in the form of answering questions about content, but in the form of performing the actual cognitive tasks the course develops. Learners observe their own cooking (M1), identify variables in their own processes (M2), explain their own outcomes (M3), design and execute their own experiments (M4), conduct their own sensory analysis (M5), troubleshoot their own failures (M6), and integrate all skills in a self-directed capstone (M7).

This principle directly informed the assessment strategy: assessments measure *performance of reasoning*, not recall of information.

**Principle 5 — Integration: Learning is promoted when learners integrate new knowledge into their everyday world.**

The capstone portfolio in Module 7 is explicitly designed as an integration task. Learners are asked to select a personally meaningful cooking challenge, apply the full range of skills developed across the course, and reflect on how their relationship with cooking has changed. The course-level reflection prompt — "What reasoning skills have I developed that I could apply *outside the kitchen*?" — deliberately pushes learners toward transfer beyond the immediate context.

The peer review activity in Module 7 also serves integration: by evaluating a peer's reasoning, learners consolidate their own understanding and see how the same principles can be applied in different contexts.

### Summary: Merrill's Principles as Design DNA

| Merrill's Principle | Where It Is Most Visible in This Course |
|--------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Problem-Centred | Module structure organised around cooking problems, not content topics |
| Activation | Module 1 (Kitchen Audit); connection prompts at start of each module |
| Demonstration | Facilitator videos, interactive walkthroughs, peer forum posts |
| Application | Every module's hands-on kitchen activities and reasoning tasks |
| Integration | Module 7 capstone portfolio; course-level reflection; peer review |

## 10.2 Technology Integration Model: TPACK and SAMR (Applied)

As detailed in Section 9, technology selection was governed by two complementary models. Here, I summarise how they informed design decisions *holistically* rather than tool-by-tool.

### TPACK: Informing the Intersection of Technology, Pedagogy, and Content

The TPACK framework was applied not as a checklist but as a design lens. At every tool selection decision, the question asked was: *Does this technology serve the intersection of our pedagogical approach (constructivist, experiential, problem-centred) and our content domain (reasoning through cooking)?*

This lens led to several specific decisions:

- **Rejecting tools that prioritise content delivery over construction.** A traditional video lecture platform was rejected in favour of Edpuzzle (which transforms video into an interactive, constructivist experience) and Articulate Rise (which enables self-paced, expandable exploration rather than linear delivery).
- **Selecting tools that support experiential documentation.** Google Classroom was chosen partly because its Google Drive integration enables seamless photo and video submission — critical for a course where evidence is generated in kitchens, not in text fields.
- **Using branching scenarios (Articulate Storyline) for troubleshooting practice.** The content domain (diagnosing cooking failures) requires a pedagogical approach (practising diagnostic reasoning with consequences) that is uniquely enabled by branching-scenario technology. This is a clear example of TPACK — the technology enables a pedagogical approach that serves the content domain in a way that no other combination could.

### SAMR: Ensuring Technology Elevates Rather Than Substitutes

The SAMR model was used as a quality filter. Tools operating at the Substitution level (merely digitising an analogue activity) were accepted only for infrastructural functions (e.g., Google Classroom as LMS). For learning activities, tools were selected only if they operated at the Modification or Redefinition level:

- **Modification examples:** Genially's drag-and-drop variable sorting modifies a classification task by making it manipulable and immediately responsive; Edpuzzle modifies video by embedding active engagement requirements.
- **Redefinition example:** Articulate Storyline's branching troubleshooting scenario redefines what is possible — learners can practise diagnostic reasoning iteratively, exploring multiple failure paths and seeing consequences, in a way that is inconceivable without technology.

This SAMR discipline ensured that technology was not added for novelty but was deployed where it genuinely elevated the learning experience.

### Integration of Both Models with Course Design

| Use Edpuzzle for facilitator videos, not plain video | Pedagogical need for active engagement in asynchronous viewing; content requires reflection, not just reception | Modification |

| Use Storyline for troubleshooting branching scenario | Pedagogical approach (diagnostic reasoning practice) uniquely enabled by technology within the content domain (cooking failure analysis) | Redefinition |

| Use Kahoot for sensory matching | Pedagogical goal of fluency-building through retrieval practice; content (sensory vocabulary) benefits from rapid, gamified repetition | Augmentation |
| Use Flourish for capstone data visualisation | Pedagogical commitment to evidence-based reasoning; content (experimental findings) is strengthened when data is made visually interpretable | Modification |

# Conclusion

This course — *The Thinking Kitchen* — represents a deliberate departure from both culinary instruction and popular food science communication. It uses the universally accessible, intrinsically motivating context of home cooking to develop transferable cognitive competencies: analytical reasoning, experimental thinking, and adaptive decision-making.

The design is grounded in constructivist learning theory, operationalised through Merrill's First Principles of Instruction, and delivered through a carefully selected technology ecosystem evaluated against the TPACK and SAMR frameworks. Every design decision — from module sequencing to tool selection to assessment strategy — has been made with a clear pedagogical rationale and a commitment to the cognitive transformation the course targets:

> **Procedural imitation → Analytical reasoning → Adaptive experimentation**

The course respects the source material (*Masala Lab* by Krish Ashok) as conceptual inspiration while maintaining strict originality in structure, sequencing, examples, frameworks, and pedagogical approach. No content has been replicated; only high-level thematic inspiration has been drawn.

The result is a seven-week, fully asynchronous, LMS-based learning experience that meets learners where they are — in their own kitchens, with their own ingredients, within their own cultural and personal cooking traditions — and guides them toward becoming thoughtful, adaptive, evidence-based practitioners of everyday cooking.

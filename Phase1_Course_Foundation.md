# PHASE 1 — COURSE FOUNDATION DESIGN

---

## 1. Course Title and Subtitle

**Title:** The Thinking Kitchen

**Subtitle:** A Blended E-Learning Course in Reasoning, Observation, and Experimentation Through Everyday Cooking

---

## 2. Course Description

Most people learn to cook by following recipes — step-by-step instructions that promise a predictable result. But recipes routinely fail, ingredients behave unpredictably, and two people following the same instructions often produce entirely different outcomes. Why?

*The Thinking Kitchen* is a six-week blended online course that uses the universally accessible context of everyday cooking to develop four transferable competencies: **structured observation, causal reasoning, adaptive decision-making, and experimental thinking.** This is not a cooking skills course. It does not teach recipes, techniques, or cuisine. Instead, it treats the home kitchen as a laboratory where learners practise noticing what they usually overlook, explaining what they normally accept, deciding what they typically default on, and testing what they ordinarily assume.

Across six modules, learners progress from recognising why familiar processes produce variable outcomes to designing and conducting their own simple kitchen experiments. The course blends self-paced asynchronous learning — video lessons, interactive activities, readings, and reflective journals — with four targeted live sessions where real-time dialogue, shared observation, and collaborative reasoning deepen understanding in ways that asynchronous work alone cannot achieve.

By the end of the course, learners will have built a Personal Cooking Experiment Portfolio that documents their journey from procedural imitation to principled, adaptive experimentation.

---

## 3. Course Rationale

Cooking is one of the few activities that nearly every adult performs regularly, yet rarely examines analytically. This makes it an extraordinarily powerful context for developing reasoning skills:

- **Universal access** — Every learner has a kitchen, however modest. No specialised equipment is required.
- **Immediate feedback** — Cooking outcomes are visible, tangible, and sensory within minutes.
- **Low stakes** — Failed experiments are edible (or at worst, harmless), reducing anxiety about error.
- **High variability** — Ingredients, altitude, humidity, equipment, and personal taste create natural variation that demands adaptive thinking.
- **Cultural resonance** — Food connects to identity, memory, and community, providing intrinsic motivation.

The course addresses a specific pedagogical gap: most informal cooking education transmits procedures ("do this, then this") without developing the principled reasoning that enables learners to adapt, troubleshoot, and innovate. The cognitive transformation the course targets is:

> **Procedural imitation → Analytical reasoning → Adaptive experimentation**

This arc mirrors higher-order thinking development across many disciplines, making the skills transferable far beyond the kitchen.

---

## 4. Course Outcomes

Upon successful completion of this course, learners will be able to:

1. **Identify and explain** at least three sources of variability that cause identical recipes to produce different outcomes.
2. **Describe** how invisible forces — heat transfer, chemical reactions, moisture dynamics, microbial activity — influence observable cooking results, using evidence from personal observation.
3. **Interpret** structural and textural changes during cooking as indicators of underlying processes, using precise descriptive language.
4. **Analyse** flavour development as a multi-variable, layered process shaped by timing, sequence, proportion, and interaction of components.
5. **Make and justify** reasoned cooking decisions when facing constraints, substitutions, or unexpected outcomes.
6. **Design, execute, document, and reflect upon** a simple personal cooking experiment, communicating findings to peers.

## 6. Delivery Model — Blended Structure

### 6.1 Overall Architecture

The course uses a **blended online delivery model** combining:

- **Asynchronous self-paced learning** (~70% of learner time) — Video lessons, interactive activities, readings, reflective journals, peer discussion forums, and at-home kitchen tasks.
- **Synchronous live sessions** (~30% of learner time) — Four scheduled 60–75 minute sessions for activities that require real-time social cognition, facilitated dialogue, or guided observation.

### 6.2 Blended Delivery Rationale

| Mode | Used For | Pedagogical Justification |
|------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Asynchronous** | Video content, readings, interactive activities, at-home kitchen experiments, reflective writing, peer forum discussions | Individual reflection, careful sensory observation during cooking, and considered written feedback require uninterrupted personal time and flexible scheduling. Adult learners need control over when and how they engage. |
| **Synchronous** | Facilitated group discussions, guided observation labs, decision-making workshops, experiment showcase | Dialogic reasoning (defending and refining thinking in real time), guided sensory attention (facilitator redirecting observation in the moment), and shared reflective experience cannot be replicated asynchronously with the same cognitive intensity. |

### 6.3 Live Session Schedule

| Session | Week | Module | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|------|--------|----------|---------|
| Live 1 | 1 | Why Recipes Fail | 60 min | Recipe failure storytelling and collaborative variability mapping |
| Live 2 | 3 | Texture & Structure | 75 min | Guided "Observation Lab" — cook together, facilitator guides attention |
| Live 3 | 5 | Decision-Making | 60 min | "Kitchen Dilemmas" — real-time decision scenarios and group debate |
| Live 4 | 6 | Experimentation | 75 min | "Experiment Showcase" — learner presentations and course closing |

**Total synchronous commitment: ~4.5 hours across 6 weeks.**

### 6.4 Weekly Rhythm

```
MONDAY          → Module materials released on Canvas (video, readings, activities)
MON–WED         → Learners engage with async content at their own pace
WED (if sync)   → Live session (scheduled evening, recorded for those unable to attend)
THU–SUN         → Complete activities, kitchen tasks, reflections, forum contributions
SUNDAY 11:59 PM → Weekly submission deadline
```

### 7.3 Navigation Rules in Canvas

- **Sequential module release:** Modules unlock weekly (Module 1 on Week 1 Monday, etc.). Learners cannot skip ahead.
- **Prerequisites:** Each module's "SUBMIT" section must be completed before the next module's content unlocks.
- **Persistent access:** Once unlocked, modules remain accessible for the duration of the course, allowing revisitation.
- **Live session pages** are pinned within the relevant module and also accessible from the dedicated "Live Sessions" navigation item.

### 8.2 Homepage Design Notes

- **Banner image:** A warm, inviting kitchen photograph showing hands at work (no faces, to maintain universal appeal across cultures). Sourced from royalty-free stock.
- **"This Week" tile:** Updated weekly by the facilitator to point to the current module — ensures returning learners immediately find where to go.
- **Progress tracker:** A simple horizontal module map using Canvas's native HTML/CSS capability or an embedded Genially graphic. Gives learners a sense of journey and accomplishment.
- **Announcements feed:** Canvas's built-in announcement widget. Facilitator posts weekly orientation messages ("This week we're exploring invisible forces — here's what to expect...").
- **"Start Here" tile:** Links to a dedicated onboarding page covering: how blended delivery works, what to expect each week, tech requirements, how to use Canvas, how to participate in live sessions, and kitchen prep guidance.
- **Mobile responsiveness:** All homepage elements use Canvas-native components or responsive embeds to ensure usability on phones and tablets (important for learners who may reference materials while cooking).

### 8.3 Onboarding Page: "How This Course Works"

Linked from the homepage, this page includes:

1. **Course rhythm explained** — Visual timeline showing weekly async + sync pattern
2. **What you'll need** — Basic kitchen equipment list, reliable internet, a device with camera (for documentation), Canvas app recommendation
3. **Blended learning guide** — "Some weeks you work at your own pace; some weeks we meet live. Here's how to prepare for each."
4. **Live session logistics** — Schedule, how to join, what happens if you can't attend (recordings + alternative tasks), what to prepare
5. **Assessment overview** — How you'll be evaluated (formative weekly tasks + summative portfolio), grading breakdown
6. **Community guidelines** — Expectations for forum discussion, peer feedback, live session etiquette
7. **Tech support** — How to get help with Canvas, troubleshooting, facilitator contact

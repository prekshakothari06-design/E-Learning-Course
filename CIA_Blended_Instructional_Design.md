# 1. Originality Statement

## 1.1 Conceptual Inspiration

The idea for this course emerged from a premise encountered across contemporary food writing — most vividly articulated in Krish Ashok's *Masala Lab* — that everyday cooking is a site of implicit scientific reasoning, sensory decision-making, and adaptive problem-solving, yet most people experience it as mere procedural repetition. The book demonstrated, through its popular-science lens, that principles of chemistry, physics, and biology operate invisibly in every kitchen.

From this premise, five high-level conceptual themes were abstracted — none proprietary to any single text:

1. **Scientific thinking embedded in ordinary practice** — the recognition that domestic routines embody testable, observable principles.
2. **Experimentation as a disposition** — the idea that deliberate variation and observation, rather than recipe memorisation, drive genuine learning.
3. **Outcome variability as a feature, not a failure** — that identical procedures yield different results depending on context, materials, and environment.
4. **Sensory perception as analytical evidence** — that sight, smell, texture, and sound constitute legitimate data when interpreted systematically.
5. **Principle-over-procedure reasoning** — that understanding *why* enables transfer to novel situations, whereas rote procedure does not.

These themes are widely represented in educational philosophy (Dewey's experiential learning, Schön's reflective practice) and in science education literature. They are not unique to any single source.

## 1.2 How Replication Was Avoided

| Dimension | Source Material | This Course |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Structure** | Organised by food-science topics (heat, spice, browning, acids, umami) | Organised by cognitive progression: recognising variability → identifying invisible forces → interpreting transformations → understanding layered processes → building decisions → designing experiments |
| **Sequencing** | Disciplinary logic following culinary chemistry categories | Pedagogical logic following learner cognitive development from observation to independent experimentation |
| **Examples** | Specific Indian recipes, author's personal cooking anecdotes | Learner-generated examples from their own kitchens and cultural traditions |
| **Frameworks** | Informal science communication narrative | Formal instructional design grounded in Merrill's First Principles, constructivist theory, and blended learning pedagogy |
| **Assessments** | Quiz-based factual recall | Performance-based authentic assessments requiring reasoning, interpretation, and experimental design |
| **Delivery** | Text-based book format | Blended e-learning with pre-recorded video, interactive digital activities, live workshops, peer forums, and reflective practice |

No chapter titles, explanations, examples, or sequencing from the source text have been reproduced. The course uses cooking as a *pedagogical context for developing thinking skills*, not as content to be transmitted.

## 1.3 Pedagogical Originality

The six module themes — from recognising recipe failure to designing personal experimentation frameworks — represent a cognitive development arc that has no parallel in the source material. The source organises knowledge by *what cooking involves* (heat, spice, acid); this course organises learning by *how thinking develops* (noticing → questioning → analysing → deciding → experimenting). The course is a reasoning skills programme delivered through cooking, not a cooking course with reasoning added.

The blended delivery model further distinguishes this course. Where the source material is a static text, this course employs a deliberately designed mix of asynchronous self-paced learning and synchronous collaborative sessions — each chosen for specific pedagogical reasons articulated in the Blended Learning Design section.

# 2. Learning Objectives

## 2.1 Terminal Objective

> By the end of this six-week blended course, learners will be able to **analyse everyday cooking processes by identifying the variables, forces, and transformations that shape outcomes, and apply this understanding to make reasoned decisions and design simple personal experiments** — demonstrating their competence through documented reasoning, reflective practice, and peer dialogue across both asynchronous and synchronous learning experiences.

**Bloom's Level:** Analyse / Evaluate / Create (higher-order)
**SMART Alignment:** Specific (cooking variables, forces, transformations, decisions, experiments); Measurable (documented outputs, reflective writing, peer engagement); Achievable (home kitchen, common materials, blended support); Relevant (everyday cooking situations for adult learners); Time-bound (six weeks).

## 2.2 Enabling Objectives

| # | Enabling Objective | Bloom's Level | Taxonomy Rationale |
|---|-------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| EO1 | Identify at least three reasons why a familiar recipe might produce variable outcomes, distinguishing between controllable and uncontrollable factors. | **Understand / Analyse** | Requires learners to move beyond surface descriptions to analytical identification of causal factors — the first step beyond procedural thinking. |
| EO2 | Explain how invisible forces (heat transfer, chemical reactions, microbial activity) influence cooking outcomes, using evidence from personal observation rather than textbook definitions. | **Understand / Apply** | Pushes learners to connect abstract concepts to concrete, personally observed kitchen phenomena — bridging theory and experience. |
| EO3 | Interpret observable structural and textural changes during cooking (e.g., colour shifts, consistency changes, volume alterations) as indicators of underlying processes. | **Analyse** | Develops the analytical habit of reading visible cues as evidence of invisible processes — a core competency for principle-based reasoning. |
| EO4 | Describe how flavour develops through the interaction of multiple components over time, and evaluate how changes in sequence or proportion alter the result. | **Analyse / Evaluate** | Moves learners from single-variable thinking to multi-variable reasoning — understanding that flavour is emergent, not additive. |
| EO5 | Make and justify a reasoned cooking decision when faced with a constraint, substitution, or unexpected outcome, articulating the principles behind the choice. | **Evaluate / Create** | The culmination of decision-making competence — learners must weigh options, predict consequences, and defend reasoning. |
| EO6 | Design a simple, structured personal experiment to test a cooking hypothesis, execute it, and communicate findings and reflections to peers. | **Create** | Requires synthesis of all prior learning into an original experimental plan, execution, and reflective communication — the highest cognitive demand. |

# 3. Module Blueprint

The six modules use the cognitive framing themes provided. Each has been expanded with a clear learning purpose, focus, targeted cognitive shift, and recommended delivery mode. Activities are kept **ideational** — directional, not prescriptive.

## Module 1: Understanding Why Recipes Fail and Recognising Variability
**Duration:** Week 1

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To disrupt the assumption that recipes are reliable instructions, and to awaken learners to the reality that cooking outcomes are shaped by variable, context-dependent factors. |
| **Learning Focus** | Recognising that identical procedures can produce different outcomes; identifying sources of variability (ingredients, environment, equipment, technique, timing). |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From "I followed the recipe, so it should work" → "Recipes are starting points; outcomes depend on many factors I can learn to notice." |
| **Enabling Objective** | EO1 |
| **Delivery Mode** | **Async:** Pre-recorded video on recipe variability; reading on procedure vs. principle; personal reflection task. **Sync (60 min):** Facilitated group discussion where learners share recipe failure stories and collectively identify variability patterns. *Rationale:* Sharing personal stories and co-constructing understanding benefits from real-time social interaction and facilitator scaffolding. |

## Module 2: Exploring Invisible Forces Influencing Cooking Outcomes
**Duration:** Week 2

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To introduce learners to the idea that cooking is governed by forces they cannot directly see — heat transfer, chemical reactions, moisture movement, microbial activity — and to develop a vocabulary for reasoning about them. |
| **Learning Focus** | Connecting observable kitchen phenomena (bubbling, browning, thickening, rising) to underlying invisible processes; developing explanatory reasoning. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From "Things just happen when I cook" → "There are underlying forces I can learn to reason about." |
| **Enabling Objective** | EO2 |
| **Delivery Mode** | **Async:** Visual explainer (animated video or infographic); interactive matching activity; guided home observation task; forum discussion. **Sync:** Not required — conceptual content benefits from self-paced reflection. Optional Q&A drop-in. |

## Module 3: Interpreting Structural and Texture Transformations
**Duration:** Week 3

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To train learners to observe and interpret structural and textural changes during cooking — treating these as meaningful data rather than incidental phenomena. |
| **Learning Focus** | Systematic sensory observation; reading texture and structure changes as indicators of process states; articulating observations in descriptive language. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From passively experiencing texture → actively interpreting structural changes as evidence. |
| **Enabling Objective** | EO3 |
| **Delivery Mode** | **Async:** Pre-recorded observation demo video; reading on structure-process relationships; sensory observation protocol. **Sync (75 min):** Live "Observation Lab" — learners cook simultaneously on video while facilitator guides attention to textural changes. *Rationale:* Sensory observation develops faster with guided, shared attention; the facilitator can redirect focus in real time. |

## Module 4: Understanding Flavour Development as a Layered Process
**Duration:** Week 4

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To move learners beyond single-ingredient thinking toward understanding that flavour is constructed through the interaction, sequencing, and layering of components over time. |
| **Learning Focus** | Multi-variable reasoning about flavour; understanding how sequence, proportion, and timing alter outcomes; evaluating trade-offs. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From "flavour comes from adding ingredients" → "flavour emerges from how components interact across time and process." |
| **Enabling Objective** | EO4 |
| **Delivery Mode** | **Async:** Visual explainer on flavour layering; comparative tasting activity (adjust one variable, describe the difference); peer discussion forum. **Sync:** Not required — comparative tasting requires unhurried, individual sensory attention. Asynchronous discussion allows more considered flavour descriptions. |

## Module 5: Building Decision-Making Ability in Cooking
**Duration:** Week 5

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To develop learners' capacity to make principled, reasoned cooking decisions under constraints, substitutions, or unexpected outcomes. |
| **Learning Focus** | Decision-making under uncertainty; evaluating competing options; justifying choices with principled reasoning; adapting to constraints. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From "I can't make this because I'm missing an ingredient" → "I can reason about what to substitute and why, based on the function it serves." |
| **Enabling Objective** | EO5 |
| **Delivery Mode** | **Async:** Interactive decision case studies; constraint challenge with documentation; reflection journal. **Sync (60 min):** "Kitchen Dilemmas" workshop — facilitator presents real-time scenarios; learners debate, justify, evaluate. *Rationale:* Decision-making under uncertainty is a dialogic skill — defending reasoning, hearing alternatives, and receiving probing require real-time interaction. |

## Module 6: Designing Personal Experimentation Frameworks
**Duration:** Week 6

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Module Purpose** | To scaffold learners into designing, executing, and reflecting on their own cooking experiments — the capstone expression of the cognitive arc. |
| **Learning Focus** | Formulating testable questions; designing controlled comparisons; documenting observations; drawing evidence-based conclusions; reflecting on the process of experimenting. |
| **Cognitive Shift** | From following prescribed activities → independently generating knowledge through self-directed experimentation. |
| **Enabling Objective** | EO6 (integrating EO1–EO5) |
| **Delivery Mode** | **Async:** Scaffolded experiment design template; home execution and documentation; structured write-up; peer review. **Sync (75 min):** "Experiment Showcase" — volunteers present experiments; group discussion; facilitator-led course closing reflection. *Rationale:* Public presentation deepens integration and metacognition; course closing benefits from shared social experience. |

# 4. Blended Learning Design

## 4.1 Design Rationale: Why Blended?

A fully asynchronous course would sacrifice two things this course cannot afford to lose:

1. **Dialogic reasoning** — The ability to construct, challenge, and refine thinking in real time with others is essential for developing decision-making and evaluative skills (Modules 1, 5, 6).
2. **Guided sensory attention** — Observational skills develop faster when a facilitator can redirect attention in the moment (Module 3).

Conversely, a fully synchronous course would sacrifice:

1. **Reflective depth** — Thoughtful written reflection, careful observation during cooking, and considered peer feedback require uninterrupted personal time.
2. **Practical feasibility** — Adult learners cooking in their own kitchens need flexible scheduling.

The blended model is therefore not a compromise but a deliberate pedagogical choice: **synchronous sessions are reserved for learning experiences that require real-time social cognition, and asynchronous activities are used for everything that benefits from individual pace, reflection, and practical kitchen work.**

## 4.2 Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Decision Map

| Module | Synchronous Component | Asynchronous Components | Justification |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------|---------------|
| **M1: Why Recipes Fail** | Live group discussion: sharing failure stories, collectively identifying variability patterns (60 min) | Pre-recorded intro video; reading; personal reflection task; post-session forum | Sharing personal failure stories and co-constructing understanding of variability requires real-time facilitation. The vulnerability of admitting failure is better supported in live, facilitated settings. |
| **M2: Invisible Forces** | Optional live Q&A drop-in (30 min, not required) | Animated visual explainer; interactive matching activity; guided home observation task; forum discussion | Conceptual content about invisible forces benefits from self-paced processing. Learners need time to digest abstract ideas and connect them to personal observation. A live session would rush this reflective process. |
| **M3: Texture Transformations** | Live "Observation Lab": guided simultaneous cooking on video call (75 min) | Pre-recorded observation demo video; reading; sensory observation protocol (post-session) | Sensory observation skills develop markedly faster with guided, shared attention. The facilitator's real-time ability to say "notice what's happening to the edges right now" cannot be replicated asynchronously. |
| **M4: Flavour Layering** | None | Visual explainer; comparative tasting (at home, self-paced); peer discussion forum | Comparative tasting requires unhurried, individual attention to sensory nuance. Asynchronous discussion allows more thoughtful, considered flavour descriptions than real-time conversation. |
| **M5: Decision-Making** | Live "Kitchen Dilemmas" workshop: real-time scenarios and group debate (60 min) | Interactive decision case studies; constraint challenge with documentation; reflection journal | Decision-making under uncertainty is a dialogic skill — defending reasoning, hearing alternatives, and receiving facilitator probing require real-time interaction. Written case studies complement this with individual depth. |
| **M6: Experimentation** | Live "Experiment Showcase" and course closing reflection (75 min) | Experiment design template; home execution and documentation; write-up; peer review | Presenting work publicly deepens integration. The course-closing reflection benefits from shared emotional and social experience. Design and execution, however, are individual, self-paced activities. |

### Summary of Synchronous Load

| Session | Module | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|--------|----------|---------|
| Live Session 1 | M1 (Week 1) | 60 min | Recipe failure sharing and variability discussion |
| Live Session 2 | M3 (Week 3) | 75 min | Guided observation lab (cook together live) |
| Live Session 3 | M5 (Week 5) | 60 min | Kitchen Dilemmas decision-making workshop |
| Live Session 4 | M6 (Week 6) | 75 min | Experiment showcase and course reflection |

**Total synchronous time: approximately 4.5 hours across 6 weeks** — manageable for adult learners with other commitments, while providing high-impact collaborative learning at the moments it matters most.

## 4.3 Module-Level Blended Design (Ideational)

### Module 1: Why Recipes Fail

- **Video:** Short pre-recorded piece (5–7 min) framing the core question: "If you followed the recipe, why did it turn out differently?" Uses relatable, non-specific kitchen scenarios.
- **Reading:** A facilitator-authored short text on the gap between procedural instruction and contextual reasoning.
- **Reflection:** Learners write about a personal recipe failure — what happened, what they thought went wrong.
- **Live Session:** Facilitated discussion where learners share stories; facilitator helps group categorise types of variability (ingredient-related, environment-related, technique-related, equipment-related).
- **Forum:** Post-session thread: "What pattern surprised you most from the group discussion?"

### Module 2: Invisible Forces

- **Visual Explainer:** Animated video or interactive infographic showing 3–4 invisible processes (heat conduction, moisture evaporation, starch behaviour, enzymatic change) using generic, non-source-specific scenarios.
- **Interactive Activity:** Drag-and-drop or matching exercise connecting observable kitchen events to underlying forces.
- **Observation Task:** Learners cook something simple at home and write a "beneath the surface" description — what they think is happening that they cannot see.
- **Forum:** Learners share observations and respond to peers with questions or alternative interpretations.

### Module 3: Texture Transformations

- **Video:** Pre-recorded demo showing how to observe and describe structural changes during cooking, modelling descriptive vocabulary.
- **Reading:** Short text on the relationship between texture, structure, and process states in cooking.
- **Live Workshop:** "Observation Lab" — learners cook a simple item simultaneously on a video call while the facilitator guides attention to specific textural changes. Learners narrate what they observe.
- **Sensory Protocol:** A structured observation sheet (digital) completed during the live session and one additional self-paced cooking session.

### Module 4: Flavour Layering

- **Visual Explainer:** Video or interactive infographic on the concept of flavour as a layered, emergent phenomenon — how timing, sequence, and proportion of components interact.
- **Comparative Tasting:** Learners conduct a self-paced at-home experiment adjusting one flavour variable (e.g., when a spice is added — early vs. late in cooking) and describe the difference.
- **Forum Discussion:** "What did changing the sequence/timing/proportion do to the flavour? What did you expect vs. what happened?" Peer responses required.

### Module 5: Decision-Making

- **Interactive Case Studies:** 3–4 written or branching decision scenarios where learners choose a course of action and justify their reasoning.
- **Constraint Challenge:** Learners select a familiar dish, impose a constraint (missing ingredient, different equipment), cook it, and document their reasoning process.
- **Live Workshop:** "Kitchen Dilemmas" — facilitator presents escalating real-time scenarios; learners debate options, defend reasoning, evaluate alternatives.
- **Reflection Journal:** "How did I make my decisions? What principles did I rely on? What would I do differently?"

### Module 6: Experimentation

- **Scaffolded Template:** Interactive experiment design form (question → hypothesis → variables → method → observation plan).
- **Execution:** Learners conduct their experiment at home with photo/video documentation.
- **Write-Up:** Structured submission: what I did → what I observed → what I conclude → what surprised me → what I would change.
- **Peer Review:** Each learner reviews one peer's experiment using a provided rubric.
- **Live Showcase:** Volunteers present experiments; group discusses; facilitator leads closing course reflection.

# 5. Assessment Strategy

## 5.1 Assessment Philosophy

Assessment in this course is built on **constructive alignment** (Biggs, 1996): each assessment directly measures the learning objective it targets, using the same cognitive activity the learning experiences develop. Learners are assessed on reasoning, interpretation, and decision-making — never on factual recall or recipe accuracy.

All assessments are **authentic** — they require learners to perform genuine reasoning using evidence from their own kitchens and experiences.

## 5.2 Formative Assessment Map

| Module | Formative Assessment | What It Measures | Aligned Objective |
|--------|---------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| M1 | Personal recipe failure reflection (written, 300 words) + live discussion contribution + forum post | Ability to identify sources of variability; willingness to question procedural assumptions | EO1 |
| M2 | "Beneath the surface" observation write-up + forum exchange (respond to 2 peers) | Quality of explanatory reasoning; ability to connect visible phenomena to invisible processes | EO2 |
| M3 | Completed sensory observation protocol (from live lab + one self-paced session) | Precision and specificity of sensory observation; quality of descriptive vocabulary | EO3 |
| M4 | Comparative tasting write-up + forum discussion contributions | Multi-variable reasoning about flavour; quality of comparative analysis | EO4 |
| M5 | Constraint challenge documentation + reflection journal entry + live discussion contribution | Decision-making quality; ability to justify adaptations with principled reasoning | EO5 |
| M6 | Experiment design worksheet (submitted mid-week for facilitator feedback before execution) | Rigour of experimental design; clarity of hypothesis and variable control | EO6 |

**Feedback mechanisms:** Facilitator provides written feedback on all formative submissions within 48 hours. Peer feedback is structured through provided protocols in Modules 4, 5, and 6. Live session contributions receive immediate verbal feedback from the facilitator.

## 5.3 Summative Assessment: The Personal Experiment Portfolio

**Aligned to:** EO6 (directly), integrating EO1–EO5.

Learners produce a **Personal Experiment Portfolio** in Module 6 comprising:

| Component | Weight | What It Measures |
|-----------|--------|------------------|
| **Experiment Design** | 20% | Clarity of question, hypothesis, variable identification, and method — demonstrating ability to structure inquiry (EO6) |
| **Process Documentation** | 15% | Quality and thoroughness of observational evidence during execution — demonstrating sensory and analytical observation skills (EO3, EO4) |
| **Results & Reasoning** | 25% | Depth of evidence-based analysis; handling of unexpected outcomes; quality of causal reasoning (EO2, EO4, EO5) |
| **Reflective Essay** (500–700 words) | 25% | Metacognitive awareness; articulation of how thinking has changed across the course; ability to connect module learnings to the experiment (all EOs) |
| **Peer Review Contribution** | 15% | Quality of structured feedback provided to a peer's portfolio — specificity, constructiveness, analytical depth (EO5, EO6) |

### Summative Rubric (Abbreviated)

| Criterion | Excellent | Competent | Developing |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Reasoning** | Evidence-based, considers alternatives, demonstrates nuanced multi-variable understanding | References evidence but may miss alternative explanations or oversimplify | Relies on recipe authority or surface-level description rather than principled reasoning |
| **Experimental Rigour** | Clear hypothesis, controlled variables, systematic observation, honest about surprises | Structured but with gaps in variable control or observation specificity | Lacks clear hypothesis or meaningful variable control |
| **Adaptive Thinking** | Principled adaptation with clear justification; reasoning transfers beyond the specific experiment | Reasonable adaptations but justification may be incomplete | Adaptations are arbitrary or poorly justified |
| **Reflective Depth** | Articulates specific cognitive shifts; connects course journey to experiment; demonstrates genuine metacognition | Describes experiences but does not fully articulate *what changed* in thinking | Descriptive rather than analytical; lists activities rather than interrogating learning |
| **Peer Engagement** | Feedback is specific, analytical, and constructive; demonstrates ability to evaluate reasoning quality | Feedback is relevant but may lack specificity or depth | Feedback is generic or superficial |

## 5.4 Constructive Alignment Summary

```
EO1 (Variability)     → Recipe failure reflection + live discussion   → M1 formative
EO2 (Invisible forces) → Observation write-up + forum                → M2 formative
EO3 (Transformations)  → Sensory protocol (live + self-paced)        → M3 formative
EO4 (Flavour layering) → Comparative tasting + forum                 → M4 formative
EO5 (Decision-making)  → Constraint challenge + reflection + live    → M5 formative
EO6 (Experimentation)  → Experiment design + portfolio               → M6 formative + summative
All EOs                → Reflective essay + peer review              → Summative portfolio
```

## Differentiation Strategy

- **Learner choice:** Learners select their own dishes, questions, and experiments — working within personal cultural, dietary, and skill contexts.
- **Scaffolded complexity:** Early modules provide structured prompts and templates; later modules progressively reduce scaffolding for greater independence.
- **Multiple expression modes:** Documentation may use text, photos, video, sketches, or combinations — accommodating different communication strengths and tech comfort levels.
- **Flexible async pacing:** All asynchronous materials available from module release; learners manage their own time within weekly windows.
- **Blended support structures:** Synchronous sessions provide real-time facilitator and peer support; asynchronous forums and facilitator messaging offer ongoing help between sessions.
- **Live session accessibility:** Live sessions are recorded and posted within 24 hours for learners who cannot attend synchronously, with alternative written reflection tasks to compensate for the interactive component.

## Notes

This course plan has been adapted for blended online delivery. Synchronous sessions are pedagogically justified (see Section 4.2) and limited to approximately 4.5 total hours across 6 weeks. All asynchronous activities are designed for completion in learners' home kitchens with everyday materials. No specialised equipment or ingredients are required.

## Differentiation Strategies for This Lesson

- **Ingredient/process flexibility:** The live lab uses a common, accessible cooking process, but learners with dietary or ingredient constraints may substitute an equivalent process (facilitator provides 2–3 alternatives in advance). The self-paced session is entirely learner-chosen.
- **Observation depth:** The sensory protocol includes both required fields (basic observations) and optional "dig deeper" prompts for learners who want to push their analysis further.
- **Expression modes:** Learners may supplement their written protocol with photos or short video clips if they find visual documentation easier or more precise than written description.
- **Live session accessibility:** The session is recorded and posted within 24 hours. Learners who cannot attend synchronously complete both Section A and Section B independently, with an additional written reflection on how the experience differed from guided observation (based on watching the recording).
- **Language support:** The observation vocabulary modelled in the demo video and live session provides scaffolding for learners who may struggle with descriptive precision, while more articulate learners are encouraged to develop their own vocabulary.

## Notes

This lesson exemplifies the blended model's value proposition: the live Observation Lab provides something asynchronous delivery cannot — real-time guided attention, shared sensory experience, and facilitator-mediated vocabulary building. The self-paced Section B then tests whether learners can transfer this guided skill to independent practice. The comparison between Sections A (guided) and B (independent) gives both the learner and facilitator diagnostic information about observation skill development.

# 8. Tool & LMS Integration Plan

## 8.1 Analytical Framework

Tool selection is grounded in two complementary frameworks:

**TPACK (Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge):** Each tool is evaluated at the intersection of technology, pedagogy, and content. The question is not "what can this tool do?" but "does this tool support our constructivist, experiential pedagogy within the specific content domain of reasoning through cooking?"

**SAMR Model:** Tools are evaluated on whether they Substitute (digitise an analogue task), Augment (add functional improvement), Modify (significantly redesign the task), or Redefine (enable previously inconceivable learning). This course prioritises Modification and Redefinition where pedagogically warranted, while accepting Augmentation for infrastructure.

# 9. Instructional Design Theory Application

## 9.1 Instructional Design Model: Merrill's First Principles of Instruction

**Merrill's First Principles** were selected as the primary design model because they align naturally with this course's commitment to problem-centred, experiential learning. Unlike process models (e.g., ADDIE), Merrill's framework articulates *what makes learning effective* regardless of delivery mode — making it apt for a blended course that must justify both synchronous and asynchronous choices against a single set of learning principles.

### How Merrill's Principles Informed This Course

**Principle 1 — Problem-Centred.**
The course is organised around progressively complex kitchen problems, not disciplinary topics. M1 asks "why did my recipe fail?"; M5 asks "how do I decide under constraint?"; M6 asks "how do I investigate a question?" Each module is framed by a problem requiring the cognitive skills being developed.

**Principle 2 — Activation.**
Module 1 is entirely dedicated to activation — the recipe failure reflection and live discussion surface learners' existing tacit cooking knowledge before new concepts are introduced. Each subsequent module's asynchronous opening connects new learning to prior module outcomes.

**Principle 3 — Demonstration.**
Demonstration takes three delivery-appropriate forms: (1) facilitator modelling via async video (M3 observation demo via Edpuzzle); (2) scaffolded walkthroughs via async interactive tools (Articulate Rise in M6); (3) live facilitator modelling in sync sessions (M3 Observation Lab, where the facilitator demonstrates real-time guided attention). This third form is uniquely enabled by synchronous delivery.

**Principle 4 — Application.**
Every module requires application — performing actual cognitive tasks, not answering recall questions. Learners observe their own cooking (M1), explain invisible forces (M2), conduct sensory observation (M3), analyse flavour (M4), make decisions (M5), and design experiments (M6). The blended design ensures application in both modes — live reasoning (M1, M3, M5) and asynchronous kitchen work and reflective writing.

**Principle 5 — Integration.**
The M6 capstone portfolio is explicitly an integration task. The reflective essay asks learners to articulate how their thinking has changed and what skills transfer beyond the kitchen. The live Experiment Showcase deepens integration through public presentation and shared course-closing reflection.

## 9.2 Technology Integration Model: TPACK and SAMR (Applied)

### TPACK as a Design Lens

The TPACK framework was applied not as a checklist but as a decision-making lens. At every tool selection point, the guiding question was: *Does this technology serve the intersection of our constructivist, experiential pedagogy and our specific content domain (reasoning through cooking)?*

Key decisions driven by TPACK:

- **Rejecting passive delivery tools.** Traditional video hosting was replaced by Edpuzzle — which transforms video into an interactive, constructivist experience — because the pedagogy demands active engagement, not passive reception.
- **Selecting tools that support experiential documentation.** Google Classroom was chosen partly for its Drive integration, enabling seamless photo/video submission — critical when evidence is generated in kitchens, not text fields.
- **Using branching scenarios for decision practice.** The content domain (cooking decisions under constraint) requires a pedagogical approach (reasoning with consequences) uniquely enabled by Articulate Storyline's branching technology. This is a clear TPACK intersection where all three knowledge domains converge.
- **Justifying synchronous tools.** Mentimeter was selected for live sessions specifically because it makes collective reasoning visible in real time — enhancing the dialogic quality that justifies synchronous delivery in the first place.

### SAMR as a Quality Filter

The SAMR model ensured technology elevated learning rather than merely digitising analogue tasks:

- **Substitution** was avoided for learning activities (accepted only for infrastructure — Google Classroom as LMS).
- **Augmentation** was accepted for supplementary tools (Kahoot, Wordwall) where the primary learning happens through other means.
- **Modification** was the target for most learning tools: Edpuzzle modifies video viewing; Genially modifies concept exploration; Nearpod modifies observation protocols; Articulate Rise modifies experiment design scaffolding.
- **Redefinition** was achieved with Articulate Storyline's branching scenarios — enabling learners to practise decision-making iteratively across multiple consequence paths, an experience inconceivable without technology.

# Conclusion

*The Thinking Kitchen* is a blended e-learning course that uses the universally accessible context of everyday cooking to develop transferable cognitive competencies: analytical reasoning, sensory interpretation, principled decision-making, and experimental thinking.

The design is grounded in constructivist learning theory, operationalised through Merrill's First Principles of Instruction, and delivered through a deliberately designed blend of asynchronous self-paced learning and synchronous collaborative sessions. Each delivery mode is chosen for specific pedagogical reasons: synchronous sessions serve dialogic reasoning, guided attention, and shared reflection; asynchronous activities serve individual experimentation, careful observation, and reflective writing.

The technology ecosystem — evaluated against TPACK and SAMR frameworks — ensures that tools enhance learning rather than merely digitise tradition. Every design decision, from module sequencing to tool selection to assessment strategy, serves the course's central cognitive transformation:

> **Procedural imitation → Analytical reasoning → Adaptive experimentation**

The course respects *Masala Lab* by Krish Ashok as conceptual inspiration while maintaining strict originality in structure, sequencing, examples, frameworks, and pedagogical approach. No content has been replicated; only high-level thematic inspiration has been drawn.

The result is a six-week blended learning experience that meets adult learners where they are — in their own kitchens, within their own traditions — and guides them toward becoming thoughtful, adaptive, evidence-based practitioners of everyday cooking.
